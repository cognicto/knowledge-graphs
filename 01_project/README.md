

The VIX (CBOE Volatility Index) models implemented in this codebase represent sophisticated mathematical frameworks for modeling market volatility expectations and their relationship to asset returns. 

The implementation consists of two primary approaches: 

1. **Mean-Reverting Jump-Diffusion (MRJD) model**   
2. **Markov Regime-Switching (MRS) model.**  
   

Both designed to capture the complex dynamics of implied volatility in financial markets. These models are grounded in the theoretical work of Heston and subsequent academic research, incorporating the fundamental insight that VIX and stock returns exhibit strong negative correlation ranging from \-60% to \-80%. 

The mathematical foundation relies on the Heston model framework where,  
 stock price evolution follows :   
dS*\_t \= rS\_*t dt  \+ √V*\_t S\_*t dW₁(t)   
and variance evolution follows :   
dV*\_t \= κ(θ \- V\_*t)dt \+ η√V*\_t dW₂(t),*   
*with correlated Brownian motions E\[dW₁ dW₂\] \= ρdt where ρ ≈ \-0.7 to \-0.8.*   
*This correlation structure ensures*  *that VIX serves as a leading indicator of market stress, rising when equity markets decline and providing crucial information for risk management and portfolio optimization strategies.* 

*The implementation emphasizes the critical timing relationship between VIX and market returns, where VIX\_*t is contemporaneously correlated with market shocks z*\_t occurring in the same time period.* *This design choice reflects empirical evidence that VIX movements and stock returns are not lagged relationships but rather simultaneous responses to common information flows and risk factors. The models incorporate shared random shocks to ensure consistency across different simulation approaches, enabling fair comparison between Pure RL and Sentiment RL strategies within the*           *Goals-Based Wealth Management framework.*   
                                                                                                                                
                                                                                                                                                                                            ***Mean-Reverting Jump-Diffusion (MRJD) Model*** **:**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   *The MRJD model represents the more academically grounded approach, implementing a sophisticated stochastic differential equation that captures three essential features of VIX dynamics:*   
*Mean* *reversion, level-dependent volatility, and crisis-driven jumps. The complete mathematical specification is given by the equation*   
***dV \= κ(θ \- V)dt \+ σ\_*****v × V^β × dW \+ J × dN,**   
where each component  serves a specific purpose in modeling volatility behavior. 

1. The **mean reversion term κ(θ \- V)dt** ensures that VIX exhibits long-term stability around its historical average θ \= 20%, with reversion speed κ \= 3.0 providing realistic adjustment dynamics observed in empirical VIX data.   
2. The **diffusion component σ*\_v × V^β × dW*** *incorporates level-dependent volatility where β \= 0.5 creates the familiar square-root structure from the CIR model, ensuring that VIX volatility increases with the level of VIX itself, preventing negative values and creating realistic variance patterns.*  
3. *The **jump component J × dN** represents perhaps the most critical innovation for crisis modeling, incorporating Poisson-distributed discontinuous moves that capture the sudden VIX spikes observed during market stress events. The jump intensity λ \= 1.5 jumps per year provides a baseline frequency that is dynamically adjusted based on market conditions, with severe negative market shocks (z\_*t \< \-1.5) tripling the jump probability to λ*\_adj \= 4.5. Jump magnitudes follow a normal distribution with mean μ\_*J \= 20% and standard deviation σ*\_J \= 15%, calibrated to match empirical VIX spike magnitudes during historical crisis periods.* 

                                                                                                                     
                                                                                                                                                                                                  
The implementation includes sophisticated numerical considerations to ensure stability and realism. VIX levels are bounded between 9% and 85% based on historical extremes, with the lower bound reflecting the lowest VIX readings during periods of extreme market complacency and the upper bound capturing crisis peaks such as those observed during the 2008 financial crisis. The level-dependent volatility term uses max(V, 1\) to prevent numerical issues when VIX approaches zero, while the jump component is constrained to be non-negative using max(0, jump) since VIX spikes are typically upward movements representing increased uncertainty rather than sudden drops in volatility expectations.                                                                               

                                                                                                                                                                                                 **Markov Regime-Switching (MRS) Model**                                                                                                                                                                
                                                                                                                                                                                                 The Markov Regime-Switching model takes a fundamentally different approach by recognizing that VIX dynamics exhibit distinct behavioral patterns across different market environments. Rather than attempting to capture all VIX behavior within a single stochastic process, the MRS model explicitly models three discrete regimes: 

1. Tranquil (low VIX environment),   
2. Turmoil (elevated uncertainty), and   
3. Crisis (extreme volatility). 

Each regime is characterized by its own parameter set including regime-specific means, volatilities, mean reversion speeds, and jump characteristics, allowing the model to capture the non-linear and time-varying nature of volatility dynamics more flexibly than traditional continuous-time approaches.                                                                                                                                                                                                     

The Tranquil regime represents normal market conditions with VIX centered around 14% and standard deviation of 3%, reflecting periods of investor confidence and stable economic conditions. The mean reversion parameter κ \= 5.0 in this regime indicates rapid return to the regime-specific mean, while jump probability remains low at 2% with modest jump sizes averaging 5%. This parameterization captures the tendency for VIX to remain range-bound during extended periods of market calm, with occasional minor spikes due to temporary uncertainty or technical factors.   
                                                                                                                                                                                                  The Turmoil regime captures intermediate stress conditions with elevated VIX mean of 25% and increased standard deviation of 6%, representing periods of heightened uncertainty such as policy uncertainty, geopolitical tensions, or moderate economic concerns. The reduced mean reversion speed κ \= 2.5 reflects the tendency for elevated volatility to persist longer than brief spikes in tranquil periods. Jump probability increases to 10% with larger average jump sizes of 12%, capturing the increased frequency and magnitude of volatility shocks during uncertain periods. This regime effectively models the transitional periods that often precede or follow major market stress events.                                                                                            
                                                                                                                                                                                                  The Crisis regime represents extreme market stress with VIX mean of 50% and high standard deviation of 15%, designed to capture periods such as the 2008 financial crisis, COVID-19 market disruption, or similar systemic events. The very slow mean reversion κ \= 1.2 reflects the empirical observation that crisis periods tend to exhibit high persistence, with VIX remaining elevated for extended periods. Jump probability reaches 20% with large jump sizes averaging 25%, capturing the frequent and severe volatility spikes characteristic of crisis periods. This parameterization ensures that once the model enters crisis mode, it tends to remain there until fundamental improvements in market conditions occur.                                                                    
                                                                                                                                                                                                  The transition matrix governs regime switching probabilities and represents one of the most critical calibration aspects of the model. The baseline transition probabilities are calibrated based on empirical analysis of historical VIX regimes, with the Tranquil state showing 88% persistence probability, Turmoil showing 65% persistence, and Crisis showing 75% persistence. These probabilities reflect the empirical observation that calm periods tend to be stable, turmoil periods are more transient, and crisis periods exhibit high persistence but eventual resolution. 

The model  incorporates dynamic transition probability adjustments based on market shocks, with severe negative shocks (z*\_t \< \-2) increasing crisis entry probability by 30 percentage points, while positive market shocks increase the probability of transitioning to more stable regimes.*                                                                                                                    
                                                                                                                                                                                                 ***Mathematical Implementation and Correlation Structure***                                                                                                                                              
                                                                                                                                                                                                 *Both models implement the crucial VIX-market correlation through careful design of the diffusion terms, ensuring that the relationship between market shocks and VIX evolution matches empirical*  *observations. The correlation implementation follows the mathematical relationship*   
***dW \= \-ρ × z\_*****t × √dt \+ √(1-ρ²) × dW*\_independent,*** *where z\_*t represents the market shock used in wealth evolution and ρ \= 0.7 represents the correlation coefficient. This formulation ensures that the same random shock z*\_t that drives negative market returns simultaneously drives positive VIX changes, creating the contemporaneous negative correlation observed in real markets.*                                                                                                                                  │ │  
                                                                                                                                                                                                    *The VIX-to-returns adjustment mechanism represents a key innovation that translates VIX levels into portfolio return modifications for wealth management optimization. The adjustment factors*  *β(VIX) and δ(VIX) modify the baseline portfolio parameters μ and σ according to the relationships*   
*μ\_*adj \= μ*\_base \+ β and*   
*σ\_*adj \= σ*\_base \- δ.*   
*The VIX-dependent adjustment factors are computed as*   
*β \= β\_*sensitivity × (θ \- VIX*\_avg)/θ and*   
*δ \= δ\_*sensitivity × (θ \- VIX*\_avg)/θ, where VIX\_*avg represents a short-term moving average that smooths out noise while preserving signal content. *The typical* *calibration values of β\_*sensitivity \= 0.04 and δ*\_sensitivity \= 0.06.*     
When VIX   exceeds its long-term mean θ, both β and δ become negative, reducing expected returns and increasing volatility to reflect the challenging market environment indicated by elevated implied  volatility. Conversely, when VIX falls below its long-term mean, positive adjustments to expected returns and negative adjustments to volatility capture the beneficial effects of low uncertainty  environments.                                                                                                                                                                                          
                                                                                                                                                                                                   
The sentiment feature extraction for reinforcement learning applications provides a three-dimensional state representation capturing different aspects of VIX dynamics.   
The features include 

1. normalized VIX level (VIX*\_current \- θ)/θ,*   
2. *normalized VIX average, and*   
3. *VIX momentum calculated as (VIX\_*current \- VIX*\_previous)/VIX\_*previous. These features are clipped to reasonable ranges to  prevent extreme values from destabilizing the learning process while preserving the essential information content needed for effective decision-making. The normalization around the long-term mean  θ ensures that the features remain stationary across different market cycles and VIX regimes.
